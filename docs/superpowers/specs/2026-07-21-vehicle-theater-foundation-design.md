# Vehicle Theater Foundation — Design

**Date:** 2026-07-21  
**Status:** Shipped and production-verified
**Branch:** `codex/military-operations`  
**Worktree:** `D:/git/ShootEM/.claude/worktrees/military-operations`

## 1. Purpose

Military Operations need battlefields sized for the vehicles that fight on
them. The current world is a fixed 100×100 grid at three units per tile: a
300-unit square. A Falcon interceptor travels 46 units per second before its
afterburner, so it crosses that world in roughly 6.5 seconds. The current
fronts work for infantry and short combined-arms encounters, but they cannot
carry a convincing jet mission, helicopter insertion, naval pursuit, or
submarine hunt.

This release establishes the spatial foundation those missions require:

- separate city, desert, countryside, mountain, coastal, and ocean theaters;
- map dimensions chosen from vehicle speed and maneuver requirements;
- four named elevation levels: Ground, Building, Sky, and Clouds;
- deterministic AI-vs-AI scenario runs with vehicle-aware telemetry;
- compatibility with every existing 300-unit map and stock game mode.

This is the first of three dependency-ordered releases. Rotorcraft are the
second release. Expanded surface craft and submarines are the third. Those
vehicles will receive their own design specifications after this foundation
ships; they must not invent competing map, altitude, or telemetry rules.

## 2. Decision and Rejected Alternatives

### Chosen: independent variable-size theaters

Each mission loads one continuous theater. The grid remains deterministic and
tile-based, but its rows and columns belong to the map instead of global
constants. The initial vehicle theater family is:

| Theater | Grid | World footprint | Primary combat |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| City | 200×200 | 600×600u | armor lanes, rooftops, helicopter masking |
| Desert | 300×300 | 900×900u | jets, armor formations, long sightlines |
| Countryside | 300×300 | 900×900u | villages, fields, roads, combined arms |
| Mountain | 200×300 | 600×900u | pass, ridges, valleys, air insertion |
| Coastal | 300×200 | 900×600u | beachhead, port, sea-to-land operations |
| Ocean | 300×300 | 900×900u | surface pursuit and future submarine hunts |

The tile size stays three world units. Buildings, roads, vehicles, projectile
ranges, and infantry do not get stretched. Bigger maps contain more geography
and longer routes, not oversized props.

A fixed-wing-primary mission requires at least one 900-unit maneuver axis.
City supports fixed-wing overflight and an authored strike approach, but never
hosts Air Superiority or free dogfighting by itself. Mountain and Coastal use
their 900-unit long axis for air approaches; Desert, Countryside, and Ocean
support unrestricted fixed-wing maneuver in both axes.

### Rejected: one all-biome mega-map

A single country-sized map would be visually impressive but would force every
mission to pay for every biome, weaken the identity of named Operation sites,
and make generation, navigation, rendering, and balance failures harder to
isolate. The campaign already selects a site; it should select the matching
theater as well.

### Rejected: linked 300-unit sectors

Sector transitions preserve current constants but introduce loading seams,
cross-sector targeting questions, and discontinuities precisely where jets,
helicopters, boats, and submarines need continuous pursuit. They defer the
real map-size work instead of solving it.

### Rejected: slow vehicles to fake distance

Reducing speed would damage the established vehicle identities and still
leave cities, mountains, coastlines, and oceans cramped. Scale belongs to the
ground, not to a global speed penalty.

## 3. Spatial Architecture

### 3.1 Map-owned geometry

`GameMap` gains a serializable geometry descriptor:

```ts
interface MapGeometry {
  cols: number;
  rows: number;
  tile: number;
}
```

The descriptor is authoritative for world width, world depth, indexing,
coordinate conversion, bounds, minimap projection, pathfinding, rendering,
spatial indexing, serialization, and validation. Shared helpers accept a map
or `MapGeometry`; consumers do not reconstruct dimensions.

Legacy maps default to `{ cols: 100, rows: 100, tile: 3 }` during load. Their
generated bytes, coordinates, balance, and behavior remain unchanged. The
existing `GRID`, `TILE`, and `WORLD` exports remain compatibility aliases for
legacy generation while consumers migrate to geometry-aware helpers. No
vehicle theater may depend on those aliases.

Rectangular maps are first-class. Mountain and coastal theaters need long
operational axes without paying for unused square corners.

### 3.2 Geometry helper boundary

One focused module owns:

- `worldWidth`, `worldDepth`, and half-extents;
- `tileIndex`, `tileCoords`, `tileToWorld`, and `worldToTile`;
- in-bounds and playable-bounds checks;
- allocation of terrain, upper-floor, and surface layers;
- iteration over cells, rims, rectangles, and neighborhoods;
- wrap or clamp against the correct X and Z extent.

Map generators, collision, projectiles, aircraft wrapping, supply drops,
spatial indexes, AI navigation, renderer terrain construction, minimap, Map
Maker, operation dressing, and validators consume this boundary. A test will
reject fixed 100/300 assumptions in files migrated for vehicle theaters.

### 3.3 Performance constraints

Only the active theater exists in a match. Generation remains deterministic
from theater id and seed. The 900×900u maps contain 90,000 cells per layer,
nine times the legacy grid, so the implementation must avoid new whole-grid
work in the frame loop.

- Terrain meshes remain batched by material/type.
- Static navigation fields are built once and cached.
- The soldier/vehicle spatial index sizes itself from geometry.
- Repeated route queries use cached fields or bounded searches.
- Generation and match-step benchmarks become production gates.

Initial budgets on the repository's normal test machine:

- 900×900 theater generation: p95 below 750 ms across 20 deterministic seeds;
- authoritative 24v24 combined-arms simulation: mean step below 12 ms and p99
  below 25 ms over a five-minute headless run;
- no unbounded growth in telemetry, path caches, projectiles, or effects.

These are engineering budgets, not promises about a specific player's GPU.
Client smoke testing must additionally show stable interaction and no visible
multi-second generation stall.

## 4. Theater Grammar

The six theaters are authored generator families, not one terrain scatter with
different colors. Every family has a strategic silhouette visible on the
deployment map and terrain laws that affect vehicles.

### 4.1 City — 600×600u

- Dense core, residential edge, rail or industrial district, and an air/armor
  approach.
- Primary roads support two heavy vehicles passing; secondary streets support
  one heavy vehicle plus infantry margins.
- Blocks create multiple through-routes and helicopter masking lanes.
- At least two wide fixed-wing approach corridors remain free of tall
  collision at Building level.
- Every objective is reachable on foot and by at least one relevant ground
  vehicle class; dead-end courtyards never receive heavy-vehicle objectives.

### 4.2 Desert — 900×900u

- Long armor lanes broken by dunes, wadis, rock shelves, and sparse compounds.
- Sightlines alternate between open fire country and terrain-masked movement.
- Two fixed-wing approach axes and one protected low-flight route are always
  present.
- Dust weather affects perception and wheeled traction without turning the
  theater into uniform blindness.

### 4.3 Countryside — 900×900u

- Fields, hedgerows, villages, farms, woods, and a road hierarchy.
- Roads are materially faster for wheels; fields remain viable for tracks.
- Villages form infantry anchors without sealing the vehicle network.
- Helicopter landing zones exist behind, on, and ahead of the main line so an
  insertion mission can express risk rather than use one mandatory pad.

### 4.4 Mountain — 600×900u

- One strategically obvious pass with at least two tactical alternatives:
  a ridge route and a valley or tunnel-side route.
- Cliffs are visible geometry and collision, never invisible blocked cells.
- Ridges create radar/AA positions and line-of-sight breaks.
- Ground objectives remain connected; air insertion can bypass a chokepoint
  but cannot land on invalid slopes or sealed summits.

### 4.5 Coastal — 900×600u

- Continuous navigable sea, beach approaches, port/channel geometry, inland
  objectives, and at least two landing corridors.
- Water routes and ground routes overlap through fire support, not through
  invalid boat pads on land.
- Shore defenses have flanking water and land counters.
- Amphibious sequencing can move from sea control to beachhead to inland
  capture in one continuous theater.

### 4.6 Ocean — 900×900u

- Open maneuver water, islands or shoals, convoy lanes, and deep-water zones.
- Surface craft always have multiple routes around hard obstructions.
- Deep-water connectivity is recorded now for the later submarine release.
- Any island objective has a valid landing relationship; infantry are never
  spawned into unreachable open water.

### 4.7 Shared generation laws

For every theater and tested seed:

- deterministic output;
- sealed or correctly wrapped domain boundaries;
- zero orphan walkable regions;
- every required objective, spawn, pad, landing zone, and door reachable by
  its declared movement domain;
- minimum lane width and turning clearance based on the largest assigned hull;
- no pads indoors, inside collision, on the wrong surface, or too close to
  another hull;
- all rendered blockers represented in collision and all collision blockers
  visually legible;
- mission manifests rejected with a descriptive error when a theater cannot
  lawfully support their required domains.

## 5. Four Elevation Levels

The existing numeric `Vehicle.band` values 0–3 become one shared semantic
type. Continuous visual height remains presentation; combat uses discrete
levels.

| Level | Name | Meaning | Normal occupants |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 0 | Ground | ground and water surface; landed aircraft | infantry, armor, boats, landed aircraft |
| 1 | Building | among structures and below/at roof country | low rotorcraft, drones, diving jets |
| 2 | Sky | clear of ordinary rooftops | attack/transport helicopters, diving jets |
| 3 | Clouds | high sanctuary and cloud concealment | fixed-wing aircraft only |

The names are player-facing and appear in controls, HUD, telemetry, tests, and
documentation. Numeric values remain on the wire for compact deterministic
snapshots.

### 5.1 Collision and weapons law

- Ground follows terrain, water, and structure collision.
- Building collides with buildings, ridges, towers, and other declared tall
  obstacles. Low flight is useful because terrain masks radar and weapons, but
  mistakes cause speed-scaled crash damage.
- Sky clears ordinary roofs but not explicit mountain/sky hazards.
- Clouds clear terrain. Cloud occupancy affects visibility and target locks;
  it does not provide immunity from air-to-air weapons or high-altitude AA.
- Ordinary ground fire reaches Ground and Building only.
- MANPADS reach through Sky.
- the shipped Lance AA track is the high-altitude SAM and, with aircraft
  weapons, can engage Clouds.
- Jets can traverse all four levels but cannot linger at Ground or Building;
  their minimum airspeed and dive timing preserve pass-based combat.
- Rotorcraft use Ground, Building, and Sky and are capped below Clouds.

### 5.2 Rendering heights

One exported table maps elevation levels to render heights. Renderer, shadows,
camera, projectile origin, cloud shelf, altimeter, and tests use the same
table. The exact values are tuned visually, but must satisfy these geometric
constraints:

- Building sits within the tall-structure collision envelope;
- Sky clears the tallest ordinary roof with visible separation;
- the cloud shelf separates Sky from Clouds;
- aircraft shadows and audio attenuation make level changes readable from the
  top-down camera.

### 5.3 Submarine depth is not negative altitude

The later naval release receives a separate `DepthState` (`surface` or
`submerged`). Reusing elevation values would conflate air collision, cloud
visibility, water depth, and weapon reach. A surfaced submarine occupies
Ground for ordinary targeting; a submerged submarine remains in the water
domain and is engaged through sonar/depth-weapon rules.

## 6. Operation Integration

Military Operation generation selects a theater family from site and verb.
Pass number affects force composition and objective complexity, not whether a
jet is squeezed into an infantry pocket.

- Air Superiority, Intercept, and Strategic Strike use Desert, Countryside,
  Mountain, Coastal, or Ocean vehicle theaters.
- Rail hub and urban seizure use City.
- Mountain pass uses Mountain.
- Beachhead, Blockade, Coastal Raid, port, carrier anchorage, and future sub
  pen missions use Coastal or Ocean.
- A small Spearhead or Siege may continue to use a legacy 300-unit front when
  no air/naval manifest is required.
- Any mission with fixed-wing aircraft, multiple rotorcraft, or blue-water
  naval objectives requires a vehicle theater.

The operation map records `theaterId` and geometry in its serializable
metadata. Briefing and deployment UI display theater dimensions, domains,
weather, and elevation access before the player commits materiel.

## 7. Vehicle-Aware AI and Telemetry

### 7.1 AI capability required by the foundation

Headless battles must be able to assign bots to useful vehicle roles and
exercise the map. The foundation supplies domain routes and tactical anchors:

- road/armor corridors and ground objectives;
- air patrol loops, approach axes, dive lanes, and landing zones;
- surface-water lanes and convoy paths;
- future deep-water patrol volumes.

Vehicle AI consumes those anchors through an interface independent of the
generator. A bot may choose another valid route, but test scenarios can order
a route deterministically.

### 7.2 Black-box extension

The current black box records on-foot crowding, stuck bodies, and ammunition.
It gains a bounded vehicle flight recorder with:

- alive, crewed, moving, and engaged counts by team, domain, and kind;
- distance traveled and commanded-versus-actual displacement;
- persistent ground, air, surface-water, and future submerged stuck incidents;
- time spent at each elevation level or depth state;
- first-contact time, time in weapons range, shots, hits, kills, and losses by
  attacker/victim vehicle kind;
- objective arrival, capture/hold contribution, cargo delivered, landing
  success, and route completion;
- crash, wrong-surface, boundary-wrap, bailout, and abandoned-hull events;
- generation seed, theater id, mission verb, manifests, duration, and winner.

The recorder is deterministic, ring-buffered, JSON-serializable, and cheap.
Its compact report highlights violations and aggregate outcome; raw data
remains available for analysis.

### 7.3 Scenario matrix

A headless scenario runner executes at minimum:

- every theater across ten fixed seeds;
- armor-versus-armor road and cross-country fights;
- jet superiority, strike run, and intercept fights;
- low-flight and Sky route probes for the upcoming helicopters;
- coastal gunboat pursuit, blockade, and beach-support probes;
- combined-arms Operation objective sequences.

The runner emits a versioned JSON artifact and a human-readable summary. Test
fixtures assert laws; balance reports describe distributions and do not hide
them behind one average.

### 7.4 Foundation acceptance bands

- zero invalid spawns, wrong-surface hulls, unreachable required routes, or
  non-finite positions across the matrix;
- zero persistent map-caused vehicle stuck incidents in deterministic route
  probes;
- every ordered vehicle reaches or meaningfully engages its assigned objective;
- fixed-wing first contact normally occurs between 8 and 45 seconds;
- ground/naval first contact normally occurs between 20 and 120 seconds;
- no team wins more than 70% of mirrored equal-manifest trials across seeds;
- every available elevation level is used in its relevant air scenario;
- the full report retains the seed and enough state to replay any violation.

A band failure blocks production readiness unless the design explicitly marks
the scenario asymmetric and pins the intended asymmetry in its fixture.

## 8. Errors and Migration

Invalid geometry fails at generation/load boundaries with the theater id,
dimensions, layer length, and violated invariant. There is no silent truncation
to 100×100 and no fallback that places a vehicle on an incompatible surface.

Legacy serialized maps without geometry migrate to 100×100×3. New maps always
serialize geometry. Unknown future geometry fields are ignored only when the
known dimensions and layer lengths remain valid. Map Maker preserves and
displays geometry; changing dimensions is an explicit new-document operation,
not an accidental side effect of import.

Network snapshots keep entity coordinates as world values. Map identity and
geometry are established with match setup; they are not resent every tick.
Clients reject a setup whose generated geometry or map hash disagrees with the
server.

## 9. Test and Verification Strategy

Implementation follows test-driven development. The minimum evidence is:

1. geometry unit tests for rectangular indexing, bounds, allocation, wrapping,
   legacy defaults, and serialization;
2. unchanged byte/determinism fixtures for representative legacy maps;
3. theater-law tests over six families × ten seeds;
4. movement-domain reachability and largest-hull clearance tests;
5. elevation transition, occupancy, collision, weapon-reach, HUD-label, and
   snapshot tests;
6. operation-selection and incompatible-manifest error tests;
7. black-box schema, boundedness, deterministic replay, and incident tests;
8. headless AI route/fight matrix with acceptance-band reports;
9. renderer/minimap/Map Maker smoke tests at rectangular 600×900 and 900×600;
10. runtime profiling against the generation and authoritative-step budgets;
11. manual deployment smoke for one City, Mountain, Coastal, and Ocean
    Operation with no console errors.

Production completion requires all repository gates:

```bash
npx tsc --noEmit
npx vitest run
npm run lint
npm run build
```

## 10. Release Boundary and Follow-on Specifications

The foundation release is complete only when the six theater generators,
map-owned geometry, four-level semantics, Operation selection, vehicle-aware
AI probes, telemetry, performance evidence, and production gates all pass.

It does **not** claim the full program goal. Two follow-on specifications are
mandatory:

1. **Rotorcraft:** a distinct attack helicopter and transport helicopter,
   flight/landing behavior, weapons/cargo, AI roles, models, pads, Operation
   verbs, and measured combat.
2. **Naval warfare:** expanded boat roles plus a playable submarine with
   surface/submerged state, sonar, anti-submarine counterplay, models, ocean
   objectives, AI roles, and measured combat.

The active goal remains open until both follow-on releases also pass their
tests, AI fight evidence, manual smoke checks, and the four repository gates.
