## 0. THE NAMING — settled

You asked what we call them in this world. **The code already answered it, and
the answer is better than one word:** there are three names for the same thing,
and who uses which tells you where they stand.

| Term | Who says it | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| **LIVING SUPER WEAPON (LSW)** | governments, the war office, the manifest | the official designation. A line item. Something a ministry funds, deploys and writes off. |
| **ASCENDANT** | everyone else | what people actually call them. The word carries awe, and it is the word the roster in the code has always used (`AscendantId`, `docs/ASCENDANTS.md`). |
| **THE BLOOD** | jackals, printers, black clinics | what they are *worth*. Not a person — a resource. |

So: **the army says LSW, the world says Ascendant, and the men hunting them
say blood.** Nothing needs renaming — the two words are a *characterisation
device* already sitting in the codebase. Use LSW in military UI (the manifest,
the officer's call, the threat table) and Ascendant in the world's voice (the
newspaper, the broadcast, civilians, the codex's prose).

The standalone game is **ASCENDANTS**. This game's war office calls the same
creatures LSWs. Both are correct, and the gap between them *is* the setting.

## 1. THE DIVERGENCE

The aliens are coming in eight years. Everybody knows. That is not the story.

The story is what humanity did with the warning.

**The Great Aliens altered human DNA** — not as an invasion, as a *survey*.
Something passed close enough to touch the genome and left. Most people were
unchanged. A small number were not, and what came out of them was not uniform:
one man could pull metal, one woman could take the sound out of a room. The
alterations were not designed. They were *side effects* — which is why the
powers are strange, specific, and rarely convenient. That is the grounding rule
for every ability in the roster: **no power was chosen, and none of them were
meant for war.** They were made into weapons afterward, by people.

Then the world did what the world does. It sorted itself into two.

**THE UNITED FRONT** and **THE COLLECTIVE** are not good and evil. They are two
answers to the same question — *what do you do with eight years* — and each one
is defensible and each one is monstrous in practice.

- **The United Front** says: *regulate, license, register.* Ascendants are
  citizens with a designation. Cloning is controlled. The state fields the
  weapon and the weapon has papers.
- **The Collective** says: *the clock is running and rules cost time.* Take
  what works. Print who you need. An Ascendant is a resource the species cannot
  afford to be squeamish about.

**Which side you fight for is decided by where you were born**, and by the time
you are old enough to have an opinion the argument is already over. That is why
enlistment reads a country's real laws at you: its government, its perception,
its cloning statute. **A nation that banned cloning made you illegal.**

## 2. THE BLOOD ECONOMY

An Ascendant's blood carries the alteration. That single fact is the engine of
the whole world.

### What the blood does

1. **It prints.** With enough of it you can 3D-print a human body that carries
   the alteration — a **PRINT**. This is the technology this entire game is
   built on: your soldier is a print, death is not the end of the account, and
   the intake line *"well… guess I'm back"* is a person waking up in a body
   that is the fourth of its kind.
2. **It accelerates.** One country — and this is the piece that made everyone
   else desperate — worked out how to use it to make things **grow faster**.
   First crops. A nation that can harvest four times a year is a nation that
   can *buy* a war. Then, inevitably, they turned it on people: a printed body
   brought to adult in months instead of decades. **A country that can grow
   soldiers faster than another country can raise them has already won**, and
   everybody could see that at once.
3. **It runs out.** An Ascendant does not produce infinite blood, and a dead one
   produces none. The supply is *people*.

### THE JACKALS

The ones without powers, who hunt the ones with.

A jackal is not a soldier and usually not a patriot. A jackal is a specialist
who crosses borders to find an Ascendant, put it down, and take what is in it
back across the border. They are the most honest people in the setting: they
never pretend it is about the war.

- They work **across countries** — an Ascendant's nationality is irrelevant to
  its blood, so a jackal will hunt in either faction's territory and sell to
  whoever is paying.
- They are why an Ascendant is never safe *at home*. The threat is not only the
  other army.
- **In-game, this is already built.** `#127 THE CAPTURE`: a fallen god's body
  lies open for thirty seconds and the blood channels out to whoever stands at
  the carcass. Every player who has ever done that has been a jackal. The
  mechanic shipped before the word did.
- **THE VAULT** — banked blood — is the jackal's ledger.

Jackal work is where the game's science missions come from: *the scientist you
extract is the one who knows how to process it.*

### The law, and the police

Printing is legal for the state and grey for everyone else. A government prints
armies. A **private** print — you, with a body you paid a jackal for — is a
crime in most of the United Front and an ordinary Tuesday in parts of the
Collective. **Do something bad in a print you were not issued, and the police
come for you.** That is the door to a whole class of missions that are not war:
warrants, seizures, black clinics, a printer working out of a garage.

## 3. WHAT THIS EXPLAINS (that already exists in the game)

The strength of this timeline is that it is not new — it is the explanation for
things already built:

| In the game | The lore behind it |
|---|---|
| PRINTS, and death not ending the account | blood-printing; you are a manufactured body |
| the intake line *"well… guess I'm back"* | she has done this before |
| #127 THE CAPTURE — bleeding a fallen god | jackal work, by another name |
| THE VAULT of banked DNA | the jackal's ledger |
| SCIENCE MISSIONS — extract the scientist | the processing knowledge is the real prize |
| cloning law per nation, in the register | the two factions' actual argument, written into 169 countries |
| a country's LSW ACTIVITY stat | where the blood is, and therefore where jackals go |
| two rosters, no heroes | born on one side or the other |
| the outbreak / reanimation layer | acceleration used on a body that was already dead |

That last row is the darkest and the most useful: **the outbreak is not a
plague, it is an industrial accident.** Growth acceleration applied to dead
tissue is exactly what a reanimation is. The zombies in this game are a
*byproduct of the economy*.

## 4. THE STORY SPINE (for the audio you want to make)

Five movements. Each one is a listening session, and each one is already
playable in some form.

**I — THE SURVEY.** Something passes. A handful of people change. Nobody
connects it for two years. Ends with the first man who realises he cannot be
hurt, and what he does about it.

**II — THE SORTING.** The world argues about what the changed people are.
Registration in one half, recruitment in the other. Ends with the first
Ascendant deployed as a weapon, and the moment the word *Ascendant* stops
meaning "blessed" and starts meaning "asset."

**III — THE HARVEST.** The blood is understood. The first prints walk. The
first crop grows four times in a year and a small country becomes rich enough
to matter. The jackal trade opens. Ends with an Ascendant killed *not by the
enemy* but by three men with a van.

**IV — THE FACTORIES.** Both sides industrialise the body. Armies of prints.
The clock — eight years — becomes the excuse for everything. Ends with the
accident: acceleration in a place it should not have been, and the first thing
that got back up.

**V — THE WAR THAT SQUANDERS THE CLOCK.** Now. Two factions burning the years
they were given fighting each other over the blood that was supposed to save
everyone. The aliens are still coming. Nobody is ready.

> **The title is the thesis.** *Consequences of Failure* is not about losing the
> alien war. It is about what humanity did with the warning.

## 5. CHARACTER SEEDS (the "grounded in something" rule)

You said the powers have to be grounded. The rule that holds the whole roster
together:

> **An alteration is a side effect, not a design. It always takes something.**

Three seeds in that shape, including the one you named:

- **THE ONE WHO EATS SOUND.** Absorbs acoustic energy — a room goes silent
  around him, and nothing he does raises an alarm. The cost: he has not heard a
  human voice since it started. He reads lips, and he is the loneliest man in
  the war. A perfect assassin and a person nobody can talk to.
- **THE FOUR-SEASON FARMER.** The one who could make things grow. Never a
  soldier. His alteration made a country rich, then made it print people, and
  he watched both happen. Alive, guarded, and the most valuable civilian on the
  planet — every jackal knows his name.
- **THE PRINT WHO REMEMBERS WRONG.** A body printed from degraded blood that
  came back with somebody else's memories in it. Legally a new person. Insists
  it is the old one. The courts have no idea, and neither does she.

## 6. OPEN — yours to call

1. **Do jackals have a faction?** My instinct: no, and that is the point. They
   are the third party, and a jackal contract board would be the best
   non-war content in the game.
2. **Can a player BE a jackal?** The capture mechanic already exists. A jackal
   career is one contract board away.
3. **How degraded can blood get?** A quality axis on the vault turns printing
   into a real economy and explains imperfect prints.
4. **Does the acceleration have a ceiling?** If a body can be grown in months,
   what does it cost the body? (A print that ages fast is a *great* clock on a
   character.)
