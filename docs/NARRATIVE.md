# CONSEQUENCES OF FAILURE
### THE NARRATIVE — five movements, written to be heard

> **How to use this.** Read it out loud, or feed it to a voice. It is not a design
> document; it is the story the design is about. One narrator carries the whole
> thing — a woman, unhurried, dry, somewhere past anger. She is not neutral. She
> just has other things to do than take sides.
>
> **The look, if you ever draw it:** warm dark. Sodium orange, rust, bone, amber
> on black. Cold light only where the machines are. Never purple.
>
> **The canon it obeys:** `THE-LORE.md` · `THREE-GAMES-ONE-WAR.md` · `ASCENDANTS.md`.

## COLD OPEN — THIRTY SECONDS

Listen.

That sound — the one like a wet drum going off inside a building — that's a god
hitting the street.

It's four in the afternoon on Northwest 183rd, and the god has come down between
a shuttered tyre shop and a house with a basketball hoop bolted over the garage.
It landed on its side. Something that big always lands on its side. Two blocks of
chain-link are lying flat in a ring around it like the grass got combed.

Nobody is looking at the sky. I want you to notice that. It comes up again later.

What they are looking at is the body, and what they are doing is counting.

Thirty seconds. That's how long one of them stays open. After that the alteration
in the blood goes flat and what's left in the carcass is worth exactly as much as
what's left in any other carcass, which is nothing, which is a smell.

So: thirty seconds, and here they come.

A rifleman named Adeyinka gets there first because he was closest and because he
has done this before. He does not put his rifle down — nobody puts the rifle down
— he tucks it under his arm like a man carrying groceries and unscrews the cap off
a steel canteen with his teeth. His squad is still shooting. The other side is
still shooting. Two men from the *other side* are running for the same body from
the far end of the street and everybody has silently agreed that the shooting is a
separate matter from the pouring.

Twenty seconds. It comes out slower than you'd think, and darker.

Fifteen. A medic gets there second and she has the good jars, the ones with the
seals, and she is furious that a rifleman with a canteen beat her to the shoulder.

Ten. Somebody's dog is barking at the sky, which is the only creature on that
street doing anything sensible.

Five. One of the men from the other side gets his container filled and gets shot
for it four steps later, and here is the thing I want you to understand: another
man from *our* side is already running for the container. Not for the man. For the
container.

Zero. The body closes. And then it is just weather, and rubble, and a hoop over a
garage, and eleven people standing around a corpse the size of a bus, breathing
hard, holding jars.

In the field manual this is called RECOVERY.

In the newspaper it's called desecration.

On that street it's called a good day, and it is — that canteen is worth more than
the house behind them.

---

It is the year 2222.

In eight years, something arrives. Everyone knows. It's not a secret, it's not a
prophecy, it's a *date*. There are calendars in kitchens with it circled. Children
have been taught the number the way my grandmother was taught her times tables.

Eight years.

And this is the story of what we did with them.

## MOVEMENT I — THE SURVEY

Nothing happened.

That's the part you have to sit with. There was no ship. No light in the sky, no
five-note chord, no man in a suit walking down a ramp with his hands up. Every
instrument humanity owned was pointed outward that night and every one of them
logged a completely ordinary evening.

A hand passed over the table and moved some of the cards.

We know it now, the way you know a thief was in your house — not because you saw
him, but because things are in the wrong drawers. Something came close enough to
read us. It took a census of the meat. It made some notes in the margin, and it
kept going.

**It did not stop.** That's the part nobody has ever forgiven. Not the changes,
not the war, not any of it — the fact that it looked at us, wrote something down,
and did not stop.

The changes were not gifts. That is the single most important sentence in this
whole history and it took the world nine years to say it out loud. Nobody was
*chosen*. Nobody was *blessed*. Something enormous and indifferent brushed the
genome the way your sleeve brushes a shelf, and afterward a few thousand people
were different in ways so specific and so useless that it took two years for
anyone to connect them at all.

A man in Antofagasta could tell you the exact weight of anything he lifted, to the
gram, and could no longer lift anything heavier than a chair.

A girl outside Kumasi began sweating fresh water — genuinely drinkable, clean —
and stopped being able to taste anything but salt.

In Narva, in a flat above a tyre yard, a woman making soup looked up because her
son had come into the kitchen, and the kitchen had gone silent. Not quiet.
*Silent.* The fridge, the road, the kettle at full boil — gone, all of it, like a
hand over the world's mouth. She thought she'd gone deaf. She screamed and couldn't
hear herself scream, and her son stood in the doorway watching her mouth move,
which is how Jaan Vesk found out, at eleven years old, that he had begun to eat
sound.

None of it was designed. That's the grounding law of everything that follows.
These were **side effects**. Nobody engineered a soldier. Something took a
sample, and the sample sloshed.

And every single one of them **took something**. Every one. There is not one
exception in the whole registry, and when the ministries finally noticed the
pattern they buried the finding for a year, because you cannot run a recruitment
poster that says *congratulations, and here is the bill.*

---

Elliot Kerr found out on a Tuesday, on the night shift, at a rolling mill outside
Hamilton.

He was forty-one. He had a wife named Maren, a bad back, and a laminated card in
his wallet that said he was qualified to weld pressure vessels. A crane let go of
a coil at the far end of the floor and the coil did what coils do, and Elliot was
where it went.

They dug him out expecting to find a stain.

He sat up. He had a hole in his coveralls the size of a dinner plate and under it
he was clean. Not healed — *unmarked*. The steel had come through him the way a
hand comes through smoke, and left him standing.

Here's what it took.

Elliot Kerr never felt anything again. Not the coil. Not heat, not cold, not
pressure, not the grain of a table under his palm, not a headache, not hunger
except as a kind of distant arithmetic. He did not feel Maren's hand.

He worked it out over the following weeks with a pencil and a mirror. Every night
he stood in the bathroom and inventoried himself, because his body had become a
thing he had to *read* rather than a thing he *was*. He would find a shirt full of
blood and have to reason backwards to the afternoon.

"Where does it hurt," Maren asked him, the first week, over and over, in the voice
people use when they don't have another question.

"That's the thing, Mare," he said. "It doesn't. It's not going to."

Ten months later a tunnel collapsed under the eastbound highway with a bus in it,
and Elliot Kerr walked into it eleven times. Eleven. He carried out nine people
and a dog and part of a tenth person, and between the eighth and the ninth trip a
news drone got footage of him standing in an active fire, patient as a bollard,
waiting for a beam to finish falling so he could get past it.

That footage is the reason for everything else in this story.

Because a great many people watched it and felt hope — and four of them watched
it and did arithmetic.

The woman who came to the house was not a soldier. She had a lanyard and a folder
and a car with a ministry plate, and she was perfectly kind. She sat in Elliot's
kitchen and admired the tile. She said the word *citizen* twice and the word
*resource* not once, which took discipline.

And her first question was not *will you help us.*

Her first question was: **"Mr Kerr — would you consent to us taking a sample?"**

He said yes. Of course he said yes; he was a good man, and it was a small ask, and
nobody had explained to him yet that in eight years' time being a good man was
going to be a supply problem.

## MOVEMENT II — THE SORTING

The argument took four years and it has still not finished. I want to be careful
here, because both sides of it were right, and I have spent my adult life cutting
the names of the dead into a wall in a town that belongs to one of them.

Two rooms.

**Room one**, somewhere with a good carpet. A licensing committee, in session
nineteen hours, arguing about a form. Their position, when you boil off the
procedure, is this: *a person is a person.* You do not conscript a citizen because
of her chromosomes. You register her. You license what she can do the way you
license a surgeon or a crane operator or a man who moves explosives through a
town. You put the weapon under the law, and the law under the people, and yes it
is slower, and yes it is a form.

They called themselves the **United Front**. Their colour is amber. Their argument
has one unanswerable line in it, and a minister named Beauchêne said it at 3 a.m.
to a room full of people who wanted to go home:

"We are eight years from meeting something that does not care what we are. I would
like it to find a species and not a stockpile."

**Room two**, no carpet, a procurement meeting, forty minutes, done. Their
position: *the clock is running and rules cost time.* You have a finite number of
months and an infinite number of committees. Take what works. Print who you need.
Sentiment is a luxury good and we are not a rich species anymore.

They called themselves the **Collective**. Their colour is cold. And their
argument has an unanswerable line too, and the director who said it was not a
monster, she was a logistician, and she said it kindly:

"In eight years there will be no licensing bureau. There will be a landing site.
I am trying to make sure there's someone standing on it."

Neither of them is the villain. I need you to hold that, because the whole war
depends on it. One of them will register you, tax you, and refuse you a permit to
do the only thing you're good at. The other will take your blood at a border
station and thank you for your service. Both of them will bury you with honours.

And here's the joke, and it isn't funny: **you don't choose.** By the time you're
old enough to have an opinion, the argument is over and you're on a side, because
of the hospital you were born in. That's all. Your country's laws pick your
faction before you can walk. Some of you were born in a place that banned cloning
outright — and that means the body you are currently standing in is illegal at
home, and your mother's country would arrest you at the airport.

Enlistment doesn't hide this from you. It reads your own nation's statutes back at
you while you sign. Government. Perception. Cloning: BANNED. It is the coldest
paperwork ever written and it is entirely honest, which is worse.

---

Jaan Vesk registered on a Thursday, in a converted post office in Narva, in a queue
of ninety people who had all been told to come at nine.

He was twenty-two. He had been eating sound for eleven years by then. He had not
heard a human voice since he was a child — he'd kept his mother's, a little, for a
while, and then that went too, and what he had left was the shape of her mouth
saying his name.

The clerk shouted at him. The clerk shouted at everyone; it was that kind of queue.
Jaan watched the man's lips and answered the questions in a flat careful voice he
could not hear himself make, and the clerk shouted louder, because people always
shout louder at the deaf as though volume were the problem, and the sound went into
Jaan and disappeared, and the room got quieter, and quieter, and the clerk began to
panic in perfect silence.

They designated him that afternoon. **ACOUSTIC ABSORPTION, PASSIVE, UNCONTROLLED.**
Class two. Licensed for civil use.

For three years the state of his birth used him beautifully. They put him on
demolition. He would stand at the edge of a condemned block at six in the morning
and take the whole charge — the bang, the roar, the long gravel sigh of a building
sitting down — and half a kilometre away a night-shift nurse would sleep through
it. He got a commendation and a small flat and a woman who learned to sign, and for
three years Jaan Vesk was the quietest useful man in Europe.

Then the war came and somebody in an office read his file and understood, in about
four seconds, that a man who deletes sound is not a demolition asset.

He is a door nobody hears open.

---

Bartol Lek was the first one they deployed on purpose, and I'm going to tell you
his story because it is the exact moment a word changed hands.

Bartol could take a word and make it true. Once a day, out loud, to everyone within
earshot. **Sleep.** **Kneel.** **Wait.** Nobody has ever satisfactorily explained
the mechanism and Bartol didn't care about the mechanism.

The cost — because there is always a cost — is that the word goes.

Not from the world. From him. Say it, and it is taken out of him permanently: he
can no longer speak it, write it, read it, or understand it when it's spoken to
him. It comes out of his head like a tooth. By the time they sent him to the bridge
he was thirty-four and had lost forty-one words, including *please*, including
*mother*, and he had learned to talk in long careful detours around the holes, so
that he sounded like a very polite man translating himself out of a language he was
still learning.

The bridge was held by nine hundred men who were not going to leave it and did not
deserve to die on it. There was a plan involving artillery. The man from the
ministry had a better plan.

"You'll say the word," he told Bartol. "You'll say it once. Nine hundred men go
home to their families. You understand what you're being offered here."

"I understand what it costs," Bartol said. "You don't. Nobody has ever been able to
tell me in advance which one it takes. It picks."

"Then it picks."

He walked out to the middle of the span at ten past six in the morning, in the
first light, with his hands where they could see them. There is a recording. There
is always a recording.

He said: **"SURRENDER."**

Nine hundred rifles went down on the deck at once. It sounded, the audio engineer
said afterward, like rain starting.

And then Bartol Lek stood in the middle of a bridge full of men laying down their
weapons and asked, with mounting confusion, what was happening. Because they were
all shouting the word back at him — *we surrender, we surrender* — and the word was
gone. It had been taken about a second and a half after he spent it. He could hear
the syllables. They meant nothing. He kept asking, and nobody could explain, because
every explanation anyone could think of used the word, and Bartol was standing
there saying *I don't understand, say it another way, say it another way.*

A sergeant finally got through to him by kneeling down and putting his rifle on the
deck and pushing it away with two fingers.

"Ah," said Bartol. "Thank you. That was well done."

The next morning, the ministry's own paperwork listed him as **LSW-004. Living
Super Weapon. Materiel class two.** A line item, in a budget, with a serial.

The newspapers called him an Ascendant.

That's the split, right there, and it never healed: the army says *LSW*, because
an LSW is something you fund and deploy and write off. The world says *Ascendant*,
because the world watched a man take a bridge without firing and needed a word with
some awe in it.

Both words are correct. The distance between them is the entire century.

He had both of them by lunchtime.

## MOVEMENT III — THE HARVEST

Elías Moreira never fought anybody. He is the most dangerous man who has ever
lived.

He was sixty-one, a fourth-generation smallholder an hour north of Montevideo, and
what the survey left in him was this: things grow around Elías. Fast. Not magic —
nothing sprouts out of the ground under his boots — but a field he walks, twice a
week, patiently, will do in eleven weeks what it ought to do in a season. Cattle
put on weight. Bad grafts take. A cracked fencepost sprouted.

The cost is the one nobody thinks about until they meet him.

Everything he touches finishes early.

He had a dog named Pancho who lived a full, happy, complete life in four years and
one month, and died an old dog, grey at the muzzle, at the feet of a man who had
loved him and had not understood yet what he was doing. Elías worked it out the
week after. He worked out the whole shape of it in one afternoon, sitting on a
bucket in the shade, and then he went and stood in the yard for a long time.

He has not held a living thing since. Nine years. He shakes hands with his elbow
turned out and a glove on. When his granddaughter was born he looked at her through
glass and cried and went home.

That's the man. Remember him kindly, because of what came out of him.

It was an agronomist named Paula Sena who found the real thing, and she wasn't
looking for it. She was trying to work out whether the effect was airborne, so she
ran controls, and one of the controls was a tray of soil with a millilitre of
Elías's blood turned into it.

The tray did in nine days what a tray does in a season.

**It wasn't him.** It was never him. It was in the blood, and the blood could be
moved, and that single Tuesday afternoon in a shed with a concrete floor is the
hinge the world turns on.

Uruguay had four harvests the following year. Then five. A country of three and a
half million people that you can drive across in a day became, in about twenty
months, one of the most consequential places on Earth, and every large nation on
the planet suddenly had a warm and pressing interest in Uruguayan agricultural
science.

To their eternal credit — and it is written on the front of their parliament, and
it is in the game's own registry to this day — they saw where it went, and they
banned it. *Cloning: BANNED.* Their motto is *Liberty or Death* and they meant it
in a room at two in the morning with the whole world's money on the table.

It was too late by about six weeks. A technician had already sold the protocol
twice.

---

Because the second thing the blood does is the thing that made all of you.

It **prints**.

Enough of it, the right machine, and you can grow a human body that carries the
alteration. A **print**. I have seen it done four hundred times and I have never
once been bored by it: the tank, the amber light, the slow assembly that looks like
nothing so much as a photograph developing in reverse. And then a person sits up in
about a foot of warm liquid and looks at their own hands for roughly forty seconds,
which is how long it takes anybody to learn a new pair of hands.

The first one who ever did it — the very first, in a hangar, with eleven people
watching and nobody breathing — sat up, looked at the ceiling, and asked for her
mother, who had been dead for six years and had never known her.

And the third thing the blood does is the reason for the war, and it is very
simple:

**It runs out.**

An Ascendant is not a well. A living one gives you a few litres a year if you treat
her gently and rather more if you don't. A dead one gives you thirty seconds and
then nothing, ever.

Which means the supply is *people*. There is no other sentence. Every refinery,
every treaty, every front line, every man with a canteen on Northwest 183rd — all
of it comes back to the fact that the most valuable substance in human history
walks around, has opinions, and can be followed home.

---

They started calling them **jackals**, and they took the name without complaint,
which tells you most of what you need to know about them.

A jackal has no power. That is the definition and the pride of it. A jackal is an
ordinary human being — no alteration, nothing in the blood worth a cent — who
crosses borders to find one of the altered and bring back what's inside them. They
work both factions, because blood doesn't have a nationality, and they'll sell to
whoever's paying, and of everyone in this whole rotten arrangement they are the
only ones who have never once pretended it was about the war.

The first was **Sandra**. She's from Los Angeles. She is unhurried in a way that
frightens people who are used to being frightened by shouting, and she wears a ring
that she talks to sometimes, quietly, when she thinks the room is empty.

And Sandra is why I have to end this chapter where I'm going to end it.

---

They took Elliot Kerr on a Sunday, from a car park, with a van and three men and
about ninety seconds of actual work.

He was fifty-one by then, still married to Maren, still doing his mirror inventory
every night. He had been what the amber side calls a Registered Civil Asset for
nine years — parades, ribbon-cuttings, four industrial fires, one earthquake. He
had a laminated card. He was, in every meaningful sense, a public servant with a
strange job.

Sandra sat on an upturned crate in the back of that van and explained the situation
to him personally, which she did not have to do and always did.

"Mr Kerr, I'm going to be straight with you, because you're the only man in this
business who can afford it. Dead, you're worth thirty seconds and a bad afternoon.
Alive, you're an annuity."

"You want to keep me."

"I want to keep you comfortable, and I want to keep you a long time. Those are the
same sentence in my line of work." She looked at him for a moment with something
that might have been the memory of pity. "You can't feel it. That's not me being
cruel, that's the actual reason it's you and not somebody else. I've done this to a
man who could feel it. I'd rather not again."

He was in that building for eleven months.

They fed him well. They gave him books and a window and, near the end, a radio,
because he asked. They tapped him twice a week and then three times, and because he
could not feel it and could not be damaged, there was no natural place for the
process to stop — no wince, no infection, no scream to tell anybody that enough was
enough. His body had no way to file a complaint. Neither did he. He was a polite
man to the end and the end was on a Tuesday, quietly, between meals, and the man
who found him had been on shift for six hours and had not noticed anything unusual
about that morning at all.

The invoice went out the same week. I've seen it. It's a normal invoice. There's a
line for transport.

The first man in history to learn that he could not be hurt.

It took the world ten years to find the one thing that could be done to him
anyway.

## MOVEMENT IV — THE FACTORIES

Here is the sentence that ended the old world, and it was said out loud in a
briefing room by a man in a good suit who was not being wicked, he was being
competent:

**"A country that can grow soldiers faster than another country can raise them has
already won."**

Everyone in that room saw it at once. Everyone in *every* room saw it at once,
which is the trouble — the idea was not secret, it was arithmetic, and arithmetic
occurs to everybody.

The blood accelerates. It had made a small country rich on wheat and beef in under
two years, and there was never any chance — *none*, don't tell me there was a
moment when we could have stopped — that a species eight years from an appointment
it did not understand was going to leave that particular tool in the shed.

So they turned it on the tanks.

A print that used to take two decades to become a soldier now took nine months.
Then seven. The halls were long and warm and lit amber and smelled like a hot
laundry, and they ran three shifts, and there is genuine industrial beauty in them
if you can turn the correct part of your brain off, which the people who worked
there mostly could.

Both sides did it. I want that clear. The amber side did it with a licence, a
registry, an ombudsman, and a chaplain in the building. The cold side did it faster
and put the savings into hulls. There is a difference between those two things and
it is not nothing. It is also not very much.

---

**Kemi Adesanya** came out of hall four in nine months and eleven days, and she is
the reason there's now a regulation.

She woke up adult. Not born — *arrived*. Twenty-two years old on paper, with a
body that had been assembled at a hundred times the speed of a childhood and a
head full of nothing at all, which they fill afterward, badly, over about six
weeks.

The acceleration did not switch off.

Nobody had thought to check, because nobody had run one long enough to find out.
Kemi ages roughly six years for every one she serves. She was three and looked
thirty. She is nine now and the medical board has her at sixty-one, and the tremor
in her left hand started last spring.

And Kemi Adesanya is the best fixed-wing pilot on her front, and she got there the
ordinary way, which I love about her: she passed flight school. She sat the exams.
She flew the hours. In this war you do not get handed the bomber because somebody
decided you'd earned it — you get handed the bomber because there is a piece of
paper with your name on it that says you are permitted to touch the bomber, and a
man in a hut who will refuse you if there isn't.

Her instructor asked her once, on the apron, why she was in such a hurry to
qualify on everything.

"Because I'm going to be dead in about two years, sir," she said, checking her
straps, "and I would like a large number of things to be true about me first."

She holds the longest confirmed strike record on the eastern board. She is
extremely funny. She does not talk about it.

---

And the clock — the eight years, the arrival, the actual reason for the entire
apparatus — became, somewhere in this stretch, a thing people stopped saying out
loud.

Not because they forgot. Because it had turned into an argument-ender. *We don't
have time* got you the budget. *We don't have time* got the ban lifted, got the
override signed, got the shift extended, got the town cleared. The most honest fact
in human history became the most useful lie in the building, and after about four
years of that, nobody could say it in a meeting without somebody sighing.

Which is how we come to the accident.

---

The plant is a rendering facility in an industrial belt I'm not going to name,
because the people who live there have had enough. It printed bodies. When a print
failed — and about one in nine did, they came out wrong, they came out empty, they
came out and did not start — the failure went down the line to disposal.

On the night of the accident there was a fault on the acceleration bus. The line
that fed growth into hall two did not shut off when hall two shut off. It kept
running, at working pressure, into the cold end of the building.

Into a room of dead tissue.

The shift supervisor was a man named Yusuf Tahan. Forty-four. Two kids. He signed
the fault log at 22:50, noted it for the morning crew — correctly, by the book,
with the reference number — and went home at eleven, and there is not one thing in
his professional conduct that anybody has ever been able to criticise.

At about half past three in the morning something in the cold room finished
growing.

That's all it is. That is the whole origin of it. **The outbreak is not a plague.**
Nobody made a weapon. Nobody opened a tomb. Growth acceleration was applied to
dead tissue by a machine that was doing exactly what it had been built to do, and
dead tissue *grew*, and what dead tissue grows into is not a person.

It is an industrial accident. It has a fault number.

By dawn it was outside the fence, and here — here is the thing that makes it worse
than any plague this species has ever survived:

**The infected cannot be printed.**

Whatever it does to the tissue, it ruins the record. It corrupts the pattern. A
soldier who dies takes a bad afternoon and wakes up in a new body two hundred
kilometres away saying something rude. A soldier who is *taken* by that thing dies
the way your great-grandmother died. All the way. Once.

We had spent nine years teaching an entire generation that death was a queue, a
delay, an inconvenience with paperwork. And then in one night we reintroduced them
to the real thing, and I watched grown veterans — men with four bodies behind them
— go completely to pieces at the sight of it, because they had genuinely forgotten
what it was.

They had to be retaught to be afraid.

That took about a week, and it is the fastest anybody has ever learned anything.

## MOVEMENT V — THE WAR THAT SQUANDERS THE CLOCK

Now.

2222. Eight years on the clock. The war is everywhere and it is between us.

---

June Alvarez came up out of the tank at 06:41 and said what everybody says, because
by the fourth time there isn't anything else:

"Well… guess I'm back."

Then she sat there on the lip of it with the amber running off her shoulders, and
she said, "Whose kitchen is that?"

They ran her print off a batch of degraded stock. It happens; the vault has a
quality axis and the good stuff goes to the fronts that are winning. What came back
was a body that worked fine and a head with somebody else's furniture in it: a
kitchen with a yellow blind, a dog she has never owned, a man's laugh she can
reproduce exactly, and a name — **Tessa Vogel** — which belongs to a woman June
Alvarez has never met and who has been dead for a decade.

She knows Tessa's mother's phone number. She has not called it. She has picked up
the handset four times.

The law is clear and the law is useless. A print is legally a new person from the
moment it stands. June is June, the account is June's, the medals are June's, and
the tribunal that heard her case sat for two days and issued eleven pages that
essentially say *we don't know either*. She insists she's the one she was. She is
also, sometimes, at three in the morning, absolutely certain she is somebody else,
and both of those things are load-bearing in a woman who is expected to carry a
rifle in the afternoon.

Her file, in the corner, has a little chip that reads the local hour and the word
for the light: **MIDDAY. DUSK. DEAD OF NIGHT.** It's the only thing in her entire
existence that has never once lied to her, and she has told me she looks at it more
than she should.

---

Here's a battle. I'll keep it short. You'll be in one soon enough.

A front on the eastern board. Amber holds two of the three grounds, cold holds the
third and won't be moved, and it has been like that for nine days.

At 14:12 the net says **POD INBOUND**, and the announcer — flat, filtered, the same
voice for both sides, which is a mercy nobody planned — reads the designation:
**THREAT THREE. SIEGE.**

And then you get thirty seconds.

That's the law of the thing and it's the best rule this war has. The bigger it is,
the longer you get to watch it coming. A skirmish-class lands in fifteen seconds. A
SIEGE gives you thirty. An EXTINCTION gives you forty seconds of everybody on both
sides staring at the same patch of ground, and there is no experience in human life
quite like standing in a ditch watching a countdown that is *also being watched by
the men trying to kill you.*

Nobody fires during it. Not out of honour. Because everybody's doing sums.

It landed. Frostbite — one of ours, the amber stable's flagship, and the coldest
thing you will ever be near — got out of the pod and the ground went white for
nine metres in every direction.

Now watch what an Ascendant actually does in this war, because it isn't damage.
It's decisions.

Frostbite put a man in a block of ice. A Collective sergeant, mid-sprint, encased
solid. And here's the thing everybody who plays this war learns in their first
month and never forgets: **that saved him.** Nothing on this earth can hurt a man in
the ice. His own squad shot the block out to free him, because the alternative was
leaving their best gun standing there safe for four minutes while the position
fell. He came out of it hurt and alive and swearing.

Freezing your enemy protects him. Timing is the entire skill. There is a moral in
that somewhere and I've never had the stomach to go looking for it.

Twelve minutes later the cold side answered with one of theirs — a duellist, a
chain, and a *mark*. He marks a man for double damage and the mark shows up on the
marked man's own screen. He *tells you* he's coming for you. That's not chivalry,
it's cruelty with a better sense of theatre, and it works: a marked man moves
differently, and everyone near him moves differently too, and the whole line bends
around one frightened human being.

Both gods died that afternoon. They always do. Every one of them dies to ordinary
rifles if enough ordinary people are willing to stand still and keep firing, and
that is the single most important design decision anybody ever made about this war,
and it was made by people who had watched what happens when a thing cannot be
answered.

Odile Harran was on that ground, and she is my last one.

Odile stops bleeding. Puts her hands on a torn man and the tear closes — genuinely,
permanently, no field dressing, no evac. She has saved more lives than any surgeon
alive.

It comes out of her. Not metaphorically. Whatever leaves them, leaves *her*: her
own volume, her own count, and it does not come back on its own. She lives at the
grey edge of it permanently. She is always cold. She sits down without being asked
to. She has fainted in four languages. And the ledger doesn't reset — every man
she's pulled back is a debit she is still carrying, so that a nineteen-year-old
medic who used to be able to save six people in a day can now save two, and knows
the number, and can tell you the day it changed.

I asked her once why she doesn't stop.

She said, "Because the ones I do are still walking around."

That's it. That's the whole ethical structure of this war, in one sentence, from a
woman who is slowly running out.

---

Afterwards it goes quiet, and everybody opens their laptop.

That's not a joke. The operations net boots up and there it is, in a warm little
window in the middle of a wrecked town: *good evening.* World status. Front Delta,
losing. Three science missions. Eighteen military. Four pieces of personal mail.
Twelve friends online, one of them from your actual hometown. The factory has your
tank. Research finished something. The promotion board says you're eligible.

Someone's headphones are playing a song that was written this morning about a fight
that happened yesterday, on a street some of them were standing on.

There's a newspaper. It prints what people actually did. It runs obituaries.

And that is where I come in, because the obituaries have to go somewhere, and I'm
the one who puts them there.

---

I keep the wall.

It's in the town, past the schools, at the end where the road stops pretending. A
long low slab, badly lit, always somebody standing at it. I cut the names of the
prints that die — not the accounts, the *bodies*. That distinction is the entire
reason the wall exists and I will fight anybody about it.

Because the account survives. Your record, your rank, your licences, your friends,
the war you fought — all of that steps into the next body and carries on, and the
army will tell you that's the same as not dying and the army is nearly right.

But that body had a life. It had a knee that hurt in the cold, and a way of holding
a cigarette, and eleven months of somebody's actual existence in it, and when it
goes it is *gone*, and the person walking around with its memories is not it. Ask
June Alvarez how firm that line is. Ask her at three in the morning.

So I cut the names. All of them, both factions, because I'm not having that
argument at a wall.

I've cut my own twice.

Nobody warns you what that's like — standing at a slab with a chisel putting your
own name into stone for the second time, at the correct alphabetical place, between
two people you knew. The first time I did it I had to go outside.

I'm running out of wall. That's my actual, daily, practical problem: I am going to
need another slab by the spring and there's a war on and the stone is going to
tanks.

---

And above all of it, every single day, unbothered, is the sky.

Eight years. The date is not in dispute. Nobody has ever suggested it was wrong.
It's in the almanac between the tide tables and the eclipse.

We were given a warning — not a curse, not an invasion, a *warning*, the single
most generous thing that has ever happened to this species — and inside of a decade
we had turned the gift into a currency, the currency into an industry, the industry
into an accident, and the accident into a reason to keep the industry running.

There are men on that eastern board who could not tell you, if you woke them at
night, what year the arrival is.

They could tell you to the millilitre what a full canteen is worth.

That's the title. That's the whole thing. **Consequences of Failure** isn't about
losing the war in 2230.

The failure already happened.

2230 is just when the bill arrives.

## WHERE THE PLAYER COMES IN

You don't pick a side. Read that again, because every other game has trained you
to expect otherwise.

You pick a **country** — one of a hundred and sixty-nine real ones, with its real
government, its real motto, its real posture toward the two things that matter in
this century: cloning, and what to do with the altered. And that country puts you
on a side. If it banned cloning, then the body you're about to be issued is illegal
at home. Nobody softens this for you. The enlistment screen reads the law out and
waits.

Then you pick a **city**, and your city is your childhood. A port town gives you
salt water and cranes, and you start knowing boats. A garrison town gives you a rifle
and a haircut. A mining town gives you charges and the dark. You begin the war
having already been somebody, somewhere, for twenty years.

Then they print you. You watch it happen — that is not a character creator, it's a
scene — and you sit up and you say the line, because everybody says the line.

**"Well… guess I'm back."**

After that, the game is three games sharing one war.

There's the **military** one, which is the loud one: fronts, armour, air, artillery,
logistics, a chain of command with real people in the seats. Command is held by an
AI and the game *tells you so*, plainly, and tells you that you can take the chair
— and if you take it, and you take it alone, you hold all three battlegrounds, and
everything that happens on them is yours.

There's the **science** one, which is small teams and no glory and matters more:
because the prize in this world was never the blood, it was the **knowledge of how
to process it**, and that knowledge lives in the heads of about four hundred people,
and both sides have lists. When you extract a scientist, you are not rescuing a
civilian. You are moving a factory.

And there's the **personal** one, which is the one you'll actually remember. Your
record. Your locker. Your dog. Your reputation on a board where people vote on
whether you deserve the seat. Your engraved rifle — and the day somebody takes it,
and the mission to get it back is not a quest, it's a *grudge*.

Progression is not a number going up. Progression is **paper**. You don't fly the
bomber because you levelled; you fly the bomber because you passed flight school and
there is a licence in your file, and the man at the gate will refuse you without it.
Certifications, rank, relationships, reputation — those are the ladder. The stats
help, and they rot when you don't use them, exactly like real skill. There is never
an aim roll. The bullet goes where you point it. What a veteran has that a rookie
doesn't is the way the gun *feels*.

You'll be a **jackal** within your first week and you won't even notice. A god goes
down, the body lies open for thirty seconds, and the blood channels out to whoever
is standing on the carcass. Every player who has ever sprinted for that has been a
jackal. The mechanic shipped before the word did. What you bank goes into the vault,
and the vault is a ledger, and the ledger is the war.

You'll **call one down**. Your faction gets one slot, live or inbound, and the
landing zone is wherever your boots are standing when you make the call. Then you
hold that ground, in the open, while a countdown runs where *everybody* can see it —
fifteen seconds for a skirmish, forty for an extinction — and if you're still
standing when it lands, the pod turns you into the weapon. Same name. Same killfeed.
Same record. One life. And when it dies, the body goes back and you redeploy as
whatever you signed up as.

You'll meet the **outbreak**, and you'll find out what it actually costs, which is
that the infected cannot be printed. The whole comfortable arrangement — die, wake
up, complain — switches off. That is the only place in this war where death means
what it meant to your great-grandmother.

And between all of it there's the **town**: schools with doors, a range, a garage, a
pro shop, a bar where the roster turns into people, a print shop where they make
another one of you, and at the end of the road, the wall.

Go and read the wall. Your names are on it.

That's the game. Every front, all three battlegrounds, and then it's over — and the
clock in the corner of your screen will tell you, in words rather than numbers, what
kind of light you spent it in.

## APPENDIX — THE SERIES

Eight episodes, drawn straight from the movements. Each one is a self-contained
story with the war in the background.

1. **THE GOOD MAN** — A night-shift welder survives a thing that should have killed
   him and discovers he can no longer feel his wife's hand. The town makes him a
   hero; a woman with a lanyard makes him a sample. *(Movement I — Elliot Kerr.)*

2. **NINETY PEOPLE AT NINE** — A deaf young man who deletes sound registers himself
   at a converted post office, gets licensed for demolition, and spends three years
   as the quietest useful man in Europe — until an officer reads his file and
   realises what a door nobody hears is worth. *(Movement II — Jaan Vesk.)*

3. **THE WORD ON THE BRIDGE** — Nine hundred men, one syllable, one take. The first
   Ascendant deployed on purpose gives up a word forever to end a siege, then stands
   in the middle of the bridge unable to understand what everyone is shouting at
   him. *(Movement II — Bartol Lek.)*

4. **FOUR HARVESTS** — An old farmer who cannot touch a living thing without
   shortening its life is discovered by an agronomist running a control tray. In
   twenty months his country is rich, and by the time its parliament bans what he
   made possible, the protocol has already been sold twice. *(Movement III — Elías
   Moreira, Paula Sena.)*

5. **THE ANNUITY** — A jackal explains her business model to a man who cannot feel
   pain, and the audience works out, several minutes before he does, why it had to
   be him. Eleven months in one building; one invoice with a line for transport.
   *(Movement III — Sandra, Elliot Kerr.)*

6. **HALL FOUR** — A woman arrives adult at nine months old, discovers the
   acceleration never switched off, and spends the two years she has left collecting
   every qualification the air force will let her sit. *(Movement IV — Kemi
   Adesanya.)*

7. **FAULT 22-50** — A shift supervisor signs a fault log correctly, notes it for
   the morning crew, and goes home at eleven. By half past three something in the
   cold room has finished growing, and a species that had forgotten how to be afraid
   of death gets a week to relearn it. *(Movement IV — Yusuf Tahan.)*

8. **WHOSE KITCHEN IS THAT** — Print four comes out of the tank with somebody else's
   memories in it, wins a tribunal that resolves nothing, and stands with a phone in
   her hand outside a house belonging to a dead woman's mother. *(Movement V — June
   Alvarez.)*

**Season finale option — THE WALL.** The keeper of the memorial runs out of stone
in a war that needs the stone for tanks, and has to decide which names get cut and
which ones wait. Framed by her cutting her own name, for the third time.
